# Chapter 2 — Theory Base of Accounting
**CBSE Class 11 Accountancy | Source: NCERT keac102.pdf (Reprint 2026-27)**
**SelfLearn study material — Sambhriti Foundation**

---

## Continuing our story…

In Chapter 1, **Meena didi** learnt to write every money transaction in her notebook. Now imagine her cousin **Suresh bhaiya** also opens a shop in the next village, and both apply for loans from the same bank. The bank manager compares their registers — but Meena counts a sale when *cash arrives*, Suresh counts it when *goods leave the shop*. Meena values her fridge at what she paid; Suresh writes whatever price "feels right" this year. **The two notebooks cannot be compared!**

This is exactly why accounting needs **common rules that everyone follows** — a *theory base*. Just like cricket is playable only because both teams accept one rulebook, accounts are trustworthy and comparable only because all businesses follow one set of accounting rules.

---

## Micro-topic 1: GAAP — the rulebook of accounting

**Simple words:** **GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)** are the rules and guidelines that all accountants follow while recording and reporting transactions, so that financial statements of different firms — and different years — can be trusted and compared.

Key points:
- They evolved over centuries from **experience, customs, professional bodies and government regulations** — nobody "invented" them in one day.
- They are **not static** — they change as law, society and business change.
- Books use many words for them — *principles, concepts, conventions, postulates, assumptions* — NCERT treats them all as one family: **Basic Accounting Concepts**.
- In India, the **ICAI (Institute of Chartered Accountants of India)** is the regulatory body that issues **Accounting Standards**.

**Why it matters:** two comparisons become possible — **inter-firm** (Meena vs Suresh) and **inter-period** (Meena this year vs Meena last year).

---

## Micro-topic 2: Business Entity Concept

**Simple words:** The business and its owner are **two separate persons** in the books — even if the "business" is just Meena didi herself.

- When Meena puts ₹50,000 into the shop, the books say: *the shop owes ₹50,000 to Meena* → capital is a **liability of the business towards the owner**.
- When she takes ₹500 home for groceries → **drawings** → the shop's debt to her reduces.
- Her personal gold necklace or house never enters the shop's books; her son's school fees paid from her personal purse is not a shop transaction.

**One-line memory:** *The shop keeps its own diary — the owner is just another party it deals with.*

---

## Micro-topic 3: Money Measurement Concept

**Simple words:** Record only what can be expressed in **rupees**, and record it in rupees — not in physical units.

**NCERT's brilliant example:** a firm owns 2 acres of land, a 10-room building, 30 computers, 20 tons of raw material, 100 cartons of finished goods. Can you add *acres + rooms + computers + tons + cartons*? No! But convert everything to money — land ₹2 crore + building ₹1 crore + computers ₹15 lakh + furniture ₹2 lakh + raw material ₹33 lakh + finished goods ₹4 lakh — and you get one meaningful total: **₹3.59 crore**. Money is the common language that lets us add unlike things.

**Limitation (exam favourite):** the value of the rupee itself keeps falling due to rising prices. A building bought in 1995 for ₹2 crore and a plant bought in 2005 for ₹1 crore are added together as if a 1995-rupee equals a 2005-rupee — which is not true. So the books may not show a "true and fair" picture during inflation.

---

## Micro-topic 4: Going Concern Concept

**Simple words:** We assume the business will **keep running indefinitely** — it is not about to shut down.

**Why this one assumption is so powerful:** it is the very reason **depreciation** exists.
- An asset is a **"bundle of services."** A ₹50,000 computer is really 5 years of computing service bought in advance.
- Because we assume the business will live to use all 5 years, we charge only **₹10,000 per year** to each year's profit — not the whole ₹50,000 in year one.
- If we could NOT assume continuity, the entire ₹50,000 would have to be charged to the year of purchase, and every year's profit figure would become meaningless.

*Shop version:* Meena didi's ₹20,000 fridge should serve 10 summers — so each year quietly "uses up" ₹2,000 of it.

---

## Micro-topic 5: Accounting Period Concept

**Simple words:** The endless life of a business is cut into **equal slices of time** (normally one year), and results are prepared at the end of each slice.

- Users cannot wait 30 years to know whether the firm is profitable — decisions are needed **at regular intervals**.
- The **Companies Act 2013** and the **Income Tax Act** require annual statements.
- **Listed companies** must also publish **quarterly** results.
- Interim (shorter-period) statements are made in special situations, e.g., retirement of a partner.

*Think of it like school exams — learning is continuous, but report cards must come every year.*

---

## Micro-topic 6: Cost Concept (Historical Cost)

**Simple words:** Record every asset at its **purchase cost** — including everything spent to make it ready for use — and keep that figure year after year.

**NCERT's data example (learn the addition!):** Shiva Enterprise bought an old plant:

| Item | ₹ |
|---|---|
| Purchase price | 50,00,000 |
| Transport to factory | 10,000 |
| Repairs to bring to running condition | 15,000 |
| Installation | 25,000 |
| **Recorded cost of plant** | **50,50,000** |

- **Why cost, not market value?** Cost is **verifiable** from documents (receipt, invoice) → objectivity. Market value changes daily and varies from person to person.
- **Limitation:** during rising prices the books understate the real worth — creating **hidden profits**. A building bought for ₹2.5 crore stays at ₹2.5 crore in the books even if the market says ₹6 crore.

---

## Micro-topic 7: Dual Aspect Concept — the heart of accounting

**Simple words:** **Every transaction has two effects**, and both must be recorded. This is the foundation of the entire double entry system.

Three NCERT examples:
1. Ram invests ₹50,00,000 → **cash (asset) ↑** and **capital ↑**
2. Goods bought for cash ₹10,00,000 → **stock ↑** and **cash ↓** (one asset swaps for another)
3. Machine bought on credit ₹30,00,000 from Reliable Industries → **machinery (asset) ↑** and **creditor (liability) ↑**

This gives the **fundamental Accounting Equation**:

> **Assets = Liabilities + Capital**

*(Everything the business owns is claimed by someone — outsiders' claim = liabilities, owner's claim = capital.)* The equation ALWAYS balances, in every transaction, in every business, every time. Remember Pacioli from Chapter 1: *"if you make one creditor, you must make some debtor."*

---

## Micro-topic 8: Revenue Recognition (Realisation) Concept

**Simple words:** Count revenue when the **legal right to receive it** arises — not necessarily when cash arrives.

- **Credit sale on 10 March, cash received 10 May?** Revenue belongs to **March** — the sale created the right to receive.
- **Rent for March 2017 received in April 2017?** Still income of the year ending **31 March 2017**.
- **Interest for April received in advance in March?** It belongs to the **next** year — a right has not yet arisen.

**Exceptions (asked in exams):**
1. **Long construction contracts** (2–3 years) — revenue is recognised **proportionately** as work completes, not at the very end.
2. **Hire purchase sales** — revenue equals the **instalments actually collected**.

---

## Micro-topic 9: Matching Concept

**Simple words:** To find true profit, put each period's **revenues** and the **expenses incurred to earn those revenues** in the **same period** — like matching each earned rupee with what it cost to earn it.

- Salaries, rent, insurance → charged to the period **they relate to**, not when paid.
- Depreciation → spread over the years the asset is used.
- **Cost of goods:** only the cost of goods **actually sold** this year is matched against this year's sales — the cost of **unsold stock is carried forward**.

*Shop version:* If Meena buys 100 kg sugar in March but sells only 60 kg by 31 March, only the cost of 60 kg goes against March's sales; 40 kg waits on the shelf (closing stock) for next year's accounts.

---

## Micro-topic 10: The "Honesty Rules" — Full Disclosure, Consistency, Conservatism, Materiality, Objectivity

Five concepts that keep accountants honest and careful:

1. **Full Disclosure** — reveal **all material and relevant facts** in the statements and footnotes. Company law even prescribes fixed formats for P&L and Balance Sheet; SEBI mandates disclosures. *Don't hide the bad news in fine print.*

2. **Consistency** — use the **same methods year after year** (e.g., same depreciation method), so years are comparable. Change is allowed, but only with full disclosure of the change and its effect.

3. **Conservatism (Prudence)** — *"Do not anticipate profits, but provide for all possible losses."* Play safe:
   - Closing stock valued at **cost or market value, whichever is LOWER**;
   - Create **provision for doubtful debts** even before a debtor actually defaults;
   - If stock's market price rises — ignore the gain until actually sold.
   - Why? Overstated profit could get distributed as dividend **out of capital** — eating the business itself. But don't overdo it: deliberately undervaluing assets creates **secret reserves**, which is also wrong.

4. **Materiality** — focus on facts big enough to **influence a user's decision**. A ₹200 stock of pencils and erasers is written off as expense immediately — nobody's loan decision depends on pencil stock. But a change in depreciation method? Material — must be disclosed.

5. **Objectivity** — every entry must be supported by **verifiable evidence** (cash memo, invoice, delivery challan, receipt) so records are free from personal bias. This is also *why* historical cost wins over market value: cost has a document; "market value" is an opinion.

## Micro-topic 11: Systems of Accounting — Double Entry vs Single Entry

| | **Double Entry System** | **Single Entry System** |
|---|---|---|
| Records | **Both** effects of every transaction | Sometimes one effect, sometimes both — no uniformity |
| Books kept | All accounts | Only personal accounts + cash book |
| Reliability | Accurate; frauds/errors can be detected; trial balance possible | Incomplete, unsystematic, unreliable |
| Who uses | Big and small organisations | Very small firms (simple and flexible) |

NCERT's blunt verdict on single entry: *"this is not a system but a lack of system."* It is politely called **incomplete records**.

---

## Micro-topic 12: Basis of Accounting — Cash vs Accrual

- **Cash basis:** record only when cash **moves**. December 2014 rent paid in January 2015 → recorded in January 2015. Simple, but it clashes with the matching concept and distorts profit.
- **Accrual basis:** record when revenue is **earned** or expense is **incurred**, whether or not cash moves. Distinguishes *receiving cash* from *the right to receive cash*, and *paying cash* from *the obligation to pay*. Gives true profit; required for companies.

*Test yourself: Meena sells ₹2,000 of goods on udhaar in March, gets cash in June. Cash basis → June sale. Accrual basis → March sale. Which shows March's true performance? Accrual.*

---

## Micro-topic 13: Accounting Standards and GST

**Accounting Standards** — written policy documents issued by **ICAI** covering recognition, measurement, presentation and disclosure.
- **Benefits:** eliminate variations in treatment; may require disclosures beyond law; enable inter-firm and intra-firm comparison.
- **Limitations:** make choosing between alternatives rigid; cannot override the law (statute).

**GST (Goods and Services Tax) — "One Country, One Tax"** (since 2017):
- A **destination-based tax on consumption** — tax accrues to the place where goods/services are finally *consumed*, and only **value addition** is taxed at each stage (with input credit for tax already paid).
- Three components:
  - **CGST** — Centre's share (replaced central excise, central sales tax, etc.)
  - **SGST** — State's share (replaced VAT, entertainment tax, luxury tax, entry tax, octroi)
  - **IGST** — on **inter-state** sales and imports; collected by Centre, then shared.
- **Worked example (NCERT):** Ramesh in Punjab sells to Seema in Punjab for ₹10,000, GST 18% → **CGST 9% = ₹900 to Centre + SGST 9% = ₹900 to Punjab**. But a sale from Madhya Pradesh to Rajasthan → **IGST 18%** on the invoice value.
- Removes the old **cascading effect** ("tax on tax"), widens the tax base, and keeps rates few.

**Memory line:** *Within the state — split the cake (CGST+SGST); across states — one integrated slice (IGST).*

## Section A — Objective (1 × 6 = 6 marks)

**Q1.** A business enterprise will not be sold or liquidated in the near future. This assumption is known as:
(a) Business entity  (b) Money measurement  (c) Going concern  (d) Accounting period

**Q2.** An old machine was purchased for ₹5,00,000. Transportation cost ₹8,000, repairs to make it usable ₹12,000, and installation ₹10,000 were also paid. As per the cost concept, the machine will be recorded at:
(a) ₹5,00,000  (b) ₹5,08,000  (c) ₹5,20,000  (d) ₹5,30,000

**Q3.** Fill in the blank: "Do not anticipate profits but provide for all possible losses" is the essence of the __________ concept, while valuing closing stock at cost or market price *whichever is lower* is its most common application.

**Q4.** State True or False: *Under the accrual basis of accounting, rent for March 2024 received in April 2024 will be recorded as income of the year ending 31 March 2025.*

**Q5.** Dealer Ramesh of Punjab sells goods worth ₹20,000 to dealer Anand of Rajasthan. GST rate is 18%. The tax charged will be:
(a) CGST ₹1,800 + SGST ₹1,800  (b) IGST ₹3,600  (c) SGST ₹3,600 only  (d) No GST on inter-state sales

**Q6.** The stock of erasers and pencils bought during the year is treated as an expense of the year, not as an asset, because of the:

Show the effect of the following on the equation: (i) Started business with cash ₹2,00,000; (ii) Purchased goods for cash ₹60,000; (iii) Purchased machinery on credit from Reliable Industries ₹1,00,000. (5 marks)

**Q10. (Case-based, adapted from NCERT Activity 1):**
Ruchica's father owns 'Friends Gifts'. When the accountant fell ill, Ruchica prepared the financial statements herself for a ₹5 lakh bank loan. The banker refused to rely on her statements after finding:
 (i) A building bought a few years ago for ₹7 lakh was shown at its present market value of ₹20 lakh.
 (ii) The method of stock valuation was changed from last year, making stock about 15% higher.
 (iii) The whole ₹70,000 spent on a personal computer (expected life 5 years) was charged fully to the current year's profit.
 (iv) She also mentioned in the accounts that "our staff is the most honest in town" and added a value of ₹1 lakh for their honesty.

Identify the accounting concept violated in **each** of the four cases, and state in one line what the correct treatment should have been. (2 marks each = 8 marks)

# Answer Key & Marking Hints

**Q1 — (c) Going concern.**

**Q2 — (d) ₹5,30,000.** Cost = purchase price + all expenses to make the asset ready for use: 5,00,000 + 8,000 + 12,000 + 10,000.

**Q3 — Conservatism (Prudence).**

**Q4 — False.** Under accrual, revenue belongs to the period in which the *right to receive* arises → rent for March 2024 is income of the year ending **31 March 2024**, even if received later.

**Q5 — (b) IGST ₹3,600.** Inter-state sale (Punjab → Rajasthan) attracts IGST at the full 18% on ₹20,000. CGST + SGST apply only within one state.

**Q6 — (b) Materiality.** The amount is too small to influence any user's decision, so strict capitalisation is not required.

**Q7 —** The going concern assumption lets us treat an asset as a "bundle of services" to be used over its life. A ₹50,000 asset with a 5-year life is charged ₹10,000 per year as depreciation, and the balance carried forward — this is fair only if we assume the firm will survive to use the remaining years. Without the assumption, the entire ₹50,000 would hit the first year's profit, distorting results. (Concept 1 mark, example 1 mark, consequence 1 mark.)

**Q8 —** *General rule:* revenue is recognised when the **legal right to receive** it arises — at the point of sale or completion of service, not on cash receipt (credit sales are revenue on the day of sale). *Exceptions:* (1) long-term construction contracts — proportionate revenue as work completes; (2) hire purchase — revenue equals instalments collected. (Rule 1 mark, each exception 1 mark.)

**Q9 —** Dual aspect: every transaction affects at least two accounts; hence **Assets = Liabilities + Capital** always balances. (2 marks for explanation.)
| Transaction | Assets | = Liabilities | + Capital |
|---|---|---|---|
| (i) Started with cash 2,00,000 | +2,00,000 (cash) | — | +2,00,000 |
| (ii) Goods for cash 60,000 | +60,000 (stock) −60,000 (cash) | — | — |
| (iii) Machinery on credit 1,00,000 | +1,00,000 (machinery) | +1,00,000 (creditor) | — |
| **Totals** | **3,00,000** | **1,00,000** | **2,00,000** |
(1 mark per transaction.)

**Q10 —**
 (i) **Cost concept** violated — the building must stay at its historical cost ₹7 lakh (verifiable), not market value ₹20 lakh.
 (ii) **Consistency concept** violated — the stock valuation method cannot be changed casually; if changed, the change and its effect must be disclosed.
 (iii) **Going concern / Matching concept** violated — the ₹70,000 computer should be spread over its 5-year life (₹14,000 per year as depreciation), not charged fully to one year.
 (iv) **Money measurement concept** violated — honesty of staff cannot be expressed in money and must not be recorded.
 (½ mark for naming the concept, ½ mark for the reason, 1 mark for correct treatment — per part.)
