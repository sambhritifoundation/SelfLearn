## The one story that runs through this chapter

Meet **Meena didi**. She runs a small kirana (grocery) shop in her village. She started it with ₹50,000 of her savings. Every day money comes in (sales) and goes out (buying stock, paying the helper, electricity). At first she kept everything "in her head." Within three months she was confused: *Am I earning or losing? How much does Ramu uncle still owe me for the groceries he took on udhaar? How much do I owe the wholesaler?*

So she bought a notebook and started writing every money transaction down, daily, in order. **That notebook habit — done systematically — is accounting.** Every micro-topic below is just one part of Meena didi's notebook system, given a formal name.

---

## Micro-topic 1: What is Accounting?

**Simple words:** Accounting is the process of **identifying, measuring, recording and communicating** the money-related events of a business to the people who need that information.

Think of it as 4 steps Meena didi follows:

| Step | What it means | Meena didi's shop |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Identify** | Pick only events that involve money | Selling rice for ₹500 → yes. A customer praising the shop → no. |
| 2. **Measure** | Put a rupee value on it | Sale = ₹500 |
| 3. **Record** | Write it in the book, date-wise | "5 April — sold rice ₹500" |
| 4. **Communicate** | Share reports with whoever needs them | Month-end: shows her register to the bank for a loan |

**Formal definitions (worth memorising for exams):**
- **AICPA (1941):** "The art of recording, classifying and summarising, in terms of money, transactions and events of a financial character, and interpreting the results thereof."
- **AAA (1966):** "The process of identifying, measuring and communicating economic information to permit informed judgments and decisions by users."

**Book-keeping vs accounting:** Book-keeping is only the *writing-down* part (steps 1–3). Accounting is the full job — it also summarises, analyses and explains the results. A book-keeper writes the diary; an accountant tells you what the diary *means*.

## Micro-topic 2: A 6,000-year-old subject (History of Accounting)

Accounting is as old as civilisation itself:

- **Around 4000 B.C.**, people in **Babylonia and Egypt** recorded wage and tax payments on **clay tablets**. Egyptian treasury keepers sent daily reports to the Wazir (prime minister), who sent monthly reports to the king — the world's first "reporting hierarchy."
- **China** used sophisticated government accounting as early as **2000 B.C.**
- **India:** About **2,300 years ago, Kautilya** (Chanakya), minister in Chandragupta Maurya's kingdom, wrote the **Arthashastra**, which described how accounting records should be maintained. So India was writing accounting manuals before most of the world.
- **1494, Venice:** **Luca Pacioli**, a Franciscan friar, published *Summa de Arithmetica* — considered the **first book on double entry book-keeping**. He gave us the terms we still use: **Debit (Dr.)** from Latin *debita* ("owed to the proprietor") and **Credit (Cr.)** from Latin *credo* ("trust/belief"). His golden rule: *"If you make one creditor, you must make some debtor"* — every entry has two sides.

**Why this matters:** kings, temples and traders all needed the same thing Meena didi needs — a trustworthy record of money. The need never changed; only the technology did (clay tablet → paper ledger → computer).

## Micro-topic 3: Economic Events — External and Internal

**Simple words:** An economic event is any happening in a business that **can be measured in money**. One event may contain several transactions.

*Example:* Meena didi buys a new refrigerator for cold drinks. The *event* = "getting the fridge ready." It includes several *transactions*: paying for the fridge (₹20,000), paying the tempo to bring it (₹500), paying the electrician to fit the plug (₹300).

Two types:
- **External events** — between the business and an outsider: selling goods to customers, buying stock from the wholesaler, paying rent to the landlord.
- **Internal events** — entirely inside the business: the store room issuing sugar sacks to the shop counter, paying wages to her own helper.

## Micro-topic 4: What gets recorded and what doesn't (Identification & Measurement)

**Rule of thumb: if you cannot put a ₹ value on it, it does not go in the books.**

Recorded ✅ | Not recorded ❌
---|---
Sale of goods ₹2,000 (cash or credit) | Hiring a very talented helper
Salary paid ₹5,000 | Helper's honesty and hard work
Purchase of a machine | A change in business strategy
Rent paid | Signing a contract (until money moves)

This is why accountants say *"the books don't show everything important — only everything measurable."* The value of a loyal customer is real, but it has no reliable rupee number, so it stays out.

**Recording** must be **chronological** (date-wise order). **Communication** means turning those records into reports (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly) and giving the **right information to the right person at the right time**.

## Micro-topic 5: Organisation and the Users of Accounting Information

An **organisation** can be any form of business — sole proprietorship (Meena didi), partnership, cooperative society, company, even a municipal body. Accounting works for all of them.

Accounting is called the **language of business** because it is how a business "speaks" about its health. Who listens?

**Internal users (inside the business):**
- Owners, managers, plant/store managers, supervisors — they need it for **planning, controlling and deciding** (e.g., Meena didi decides whether to stock more cold drinks in summer).

**External users (outside the business):**
- **Investors** — is my money earning a good return?
- **Banks/creditors** — will they be able to repay the loan? (When Meena didi asks for a ₹1 lakh loan, the bank manager asks for her accounts.)
- **Government/tax authorities** — is the right tax being paid?
- **Employees/unions** — is the firm stable? Can it pay bonus?
- **Customers** — will this business survive so my supply/service continues?
- **Regulators (SEBI, Registrar of Companies)** — are rules being followed?

## Micro-topic 6: Branches of Accounting

As businesses grew, one accountant's job split into three specialisations:

1. **Financial Accounting** — records all transactions to find **profit/loss** and **financial position**. Looks at the **past**. For everyone (owners + outsiders).
2. **Cost Accounting** — finds the **cost of each product/service** so prices can be set and costs controlled. (*What does one plate of samosas actually cost the shop?*)
3. **Management Accounting** — gives managers information for **future decisions**: budgets, sales forecasts, cash-flow plans.

Memory trick: **Financial = past scorecard, Cost = price tag, Management = future GPS.**

Modern extensions: forensic accounting (fraud detection), environmental accounting, human resource accounting, social accounting.

## Micro-topic 7: Qualitative Characteristics of Accounting Information

Good accounting information must have 4 qualities. Think of a **news report** — you'd want it truthful, timely, clear and comparable. Same here:

1. **Reliability** — free from error and bias; verifiable. If two independent people check the record, they should reach the same figure. (The ₹500 sale has a bill to prove it.)
2. **Relevance** — available **in time** and useful for the decision at hand. Building-price data from 1998 is useless for buying a building today; last month's price is relevant.
3. **Understandability** — presented so the reader understands it exactly as the preparer meant. No confusion, no jargon-fog.
4. **Comparability** — same period, same units, same format — so you can compare this year vs last year, or your shop vs another shop.

Memory trick: **"R R U C"** — *Right (reliable), Relevant, Understandable, Comparable.*

## Micro-topic 8: Objectives of Accounting

Four main objectives — all four are literally why Meena didi keeps her notebook:

1. **Maintain systematic records** — no human memory can hold hundreds of transactions. Records also act as **evidence**.
2. **Calculate profit or loss** — Profit = Revenue − Expenses.
   *NCERT's own example:* Revenue ₹6,00,000 − Expenses ₹5,40,000 = **Profit ₹60,000**. If expenses were higher, the difference would be a loss.
3. **Show the financial position** — a **Balance Sheet** lists what the business *owns* (assets) and what it *owes* (liabilities) on a date. It is the business's "health check-up report."
4. **Provide information to users** — reports, statements, charts for both internal and external users.

---

## Micro-topic 9: Role and Limitations of Accounting

Accounting plays five roles (Box 4 of NCERT — frequent exam question):

- **A language** — the language of business.
- **A historical record** — chronological record of actual transactions at actual amounts.
- **Current economic reality** — a way to measure the true change in wealth over time.
- **An information system** — links the source (accountant) to receivers (users) through reports.
- **A commodity/service** — specialised information that society demands.

**Limitations (must-write in long answers):** it records only **past**, **quantitative**, **financial** information — it ignores qualitative things (staff morale, customer loyalty) and non-financial facts.

## Micro-topic 10: Basic Terms in Accounting (the vocabulary list)

Learn these as one family, using Meena didi's shop:

| Term | Simple meaning | Shop example |
|---|---|---|
| **Entity** | The business as a separate identity | "Meena Kirana Store" is separate from Meena the person |
| **Transaction** | An event involving value between two parties | Selling biscuits for ₹40 |
| **Capital** | Owner's money put into the business (shown as a liability — the business "owes" the owner) | Her starting ₹50,000 |
| **Drawings** | Money/goods the owner takes out for personal use (reduces capital) | She takes ₹500 + a soap for home |
| **Assets** | Valuable things the business owns. **Non-current/Fixed** (long life): shop furniture, fridge. **Current** (within 12 months): cash, stock, debtors | Fridge ₹20,000; cash ₹8,000 |
| **Liabilities** | What the business owes to outsiders. **Non-current** (loans over a year), **Current** (payable within 12 months) | Bank loan; wholesaler's bill |
| **Sales** | Revenue from selling goods/services (cash or credit) | Day's sales ₹3,000 |
| **Revenue/Income** | Money earned from business activities: sales, commission, interest, rent received | Sales + ₹200 interest from bank |
| **Expenses** | Costs incurred to earn revenue: rent, wages, electricity, depreciation | Helper's wages ₹3,000/month |
| **Expenditure** | Any spending. Benefit used up within a year = **revenue expenditure** (expense); benefit lasting years = **capital expenditure** (asset) | Electricity bill = revenue exp.; fridge = capital exp. |
| **Profit** | Revenue − Expenses (from normal business) | Earned ₹9,000 over the month's costs |
| **Gain** | Profit from something *incidental* (not the main business) | Sold old furniture costing ₹4,000 for ₹4,500 → gain ₹500 |
| **Loss** | Expenses exceed revenue; or money lost with no benefit | Stock worth ₹3,000 destroyed by rats/fire |
| **Discount** | Reduction in price. **Trade discount** — off the list price at time of sale. **Cash discount** — reward for paying early | 10% off MRP for a bulk buyer (trade); "pay this week and I'll cut ₹50" (cash) |
| **Voucher** | The documentary proof of a transaction | Cash memo, invoice, receipt |
| **Goods** | The products the business deals in | For her: groceries. (Her fridge is NOT goods — it's an asset. But for a fridge dealer, fridges ARE goods!) |
| **Purchases** | Goods bought (cash or credit) for resale/use | Buying sugar sacks from the wholesaler |
| **Stock/Inventory** | Unsold goods on hand at a date (opening and closing stock) | Goods on shelves on 31 March |
| **Debtors** | People who owe money *to* the business (asset) | Ramu uncle's udhaar of ₹800 |
| **Creditors** | People the business owes money *to* (liability) | Wholesaler waiting for ₹5,000 |

**Debtor vs creditor in one line:** *Debtor Deta hai (will give us), Creditor ko Chukana hai (we must pay).*

## Section A — Objective (1 × 6 = 6 marks)

**Q1.** Which of the following is the correct sequence of the accounting process?
(a) Recording → Identifying → Measuring → Communicating
(b) Identifying → Measuring → Recording → Communicating
(c) Measuring → Recording → Identifying → Communicating
(d) Identifying → Recording → Measuring → Communicating

**Q2.** Which of the following will **not** be recorded in the books of account?
(a) Purchase of a delivery van for ₹3,00,000
(b) Payment of salary ₹15,000
(c) Appointment of a new, highly experienced manager
(d) Goods lost by fire ₹10,000

**Q3.** State whether True or False: *"Capital is shown on the liabilities side of the balance sheet because the business owes this amount to the owner."*

**Q4.** Fill in the blank: Discount allowed as a deduction from the list price at the time of sale is called __________ discount, while discount allowed for prompt payment is called __________ discount.

**Q5.** Which qualitative characteristic of accounting information is violated if the annual reports of a firm use a different reporting format every year, making year-to-year analysis impossible?
(a) Reliability  (b) Relevance  (c) Understandability  (d) Comparability

**Q6.** Rahul, a furniture dealer, purchased 10 chairs for resale and 1 computer for maintaining his accounts. In his books:
(a) Both are goods  (b) Both are assets
(c) Chairs are goods; the computer is an asset  (d) Chairs are an asset; the computer is goods

## Section B — Short Answer (3 × 2 = 6 marks)

**Q7.** Distinguish between *debtors* and *creditors* (any three points), giving one example of each.

**Q8.** "Profit and gain are the same thing." Do you agree? Explain with one example of each.

## Section C — Long Answer (5 + 8 = 13 marks)

**Q9.** Explain the four qualitative characteristics of accounting information which make it useful for decision-making. (5 marks)

**Q10. (Case-based, like NCERT Test Your Understanding V):**
Mr. Aman started a mobile-accessories business with ₹4,00,000 as initial investment. Out of this he paid ₹80,000 for furniture and ₹2,00,000 for buying accessories (goods). He employed a salesperson and paid ₹6,000 as salary for the month. He sold accessories for ₹1,20,000 in cash and accessories worth ₹90,000 on credit to Mr. Bharat. He then bought more accessories worth ₹1,00,000 on credit from M/s Chetan Traders. Due to a short-circuit, accessories worth ₹25,000 were destroyed. He sold his old scooter (personal decision to use business funds is not involved; the scooter belonged to the business, costing ₹30,000) for ₹34,000.

From the above, answer: (1 × 8 = 8 marks)
 (i) What is Aman's capital?
 (ii) What are his fixed assets?
 (iii) What is the total value of goods purchased?
 (iv) Who is the debtor and how much does he owe?
 (v) Who is the creditor and how much is payable?
 (vi) What are the expenses?
 (vii) What is the gain?
 (viii) What is the loss?

# Answer Key & Marking Hints

**Q1 — (b).** Identify money-events first, measure them in ₹, record date-wise, then communicate through reports.

**Q2 — (c).** The appointment of a manager cannot be measured in money (Money Measurement); all other options have clear rupee values.

**Q3 — True.** Under the entity concept the business is separate from the owner, so the owner's capital is a claim (internal liability) against the business.

**Q4 — Trade discount; Cash discount.**

**Q5 — (d) Comparability.** Comparison requires a common period, common measurement unit and common format.

**Q6 — (c).** "Goods" are only the items a business deals in for resale. Chairs are for resale (goods); the computer is for use in business (asset).

**Q7 — Debtors vs Creditors (any 3):**
| Basis | Debtors | Creditors |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Owe money **to** the business | Business owes money **to** them |
| Arise from | Credit **sales** | Credit **purchases** |
| Balance sheet | Asset side (sundry debtors) | Liabilities side (sundry creditors) |
| Example | Customer who took goods on udhaar | Wholesaler yet to be paid |

**Q8 — No, they differ.** *Profit* arises from the **normal operations** of business (Revenue − Expenses, e.g., a grocer earning ₹60,000 over the year from selling groceries). *Gain* arises from **incidental** events — e.g., selling an old machine costing ₹40,000 for ₹45,000 (gain ₹5,000), winning a court case, or appreciation of an asset's value. (1 mark for disagreeing with reason, 1 mark each for correct examples.)

**Q9 —** Explain each with a line of meaning + one example (1 mark each + 1 for presentation):
1. *Reliability* — free from error/bias, verifiable, faithful representation.
2. *Relevance* — timely, helps in prediction and feedback of decisions.
3. *Understandability* — receiver interprets the message exactly as intended.
4. *Comparability* — common period, unit and format allow comparison across years and firms.

**Q10 —**
 (i) Capital = **₹4,00,000**
 (ii) Fixed assets = **Furniture ₹80,000** (the scooter ₹30,000 was also a fixed asset until sold)
 (iii) Goods purchased = ₹2,00,000 + ₹1,00,000 = **₹3,00,000**
 (iv) Debtor = **Mr. Bharat, ₹90,000**
 (v) Creditor = **M/s Chetan Traders, ₹1,00,000**
 (vi) Expenses = **Salary ₹6,000**
 (vii) Gain = ₹34,000 − ₹30,000 = **₹4,000** (incidental sale of a fixed asset)
 (viii) Loss = **₹25,000** (goods destroyed by short-circuit — no benefit received)
